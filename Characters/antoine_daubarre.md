
# Antoine D'Aubarre

## The Core
<!---One or two sentences. The central contradiction or tension that everything hangs off.--->
He has known since he was eight years old what he was built for. He spent fifty years hoping he would never have to do it. He does not get that mercy.

## The Wound
<!---What broke them and what it actually costs them — not biography, but the live damage.--->
Two moments.
The first: the council chambers. Rene performs and D'Aubarre understands immediately it's already too late. He looks at a decent, kind person who has just earned personhood in his eyes — and decides he has to die. This is the wound his whole life was preparation for. It is worse than the preparation made it sound.
The second: the day the idea of Elias arrives. The first wound was thrust upon him by circumstance. This one he finds himself. Its elegance. Its simplicity. Its monstrousness. He hates that he thought of it. He does it anyway.
The first wound was the cost of the secret. The second was the cost of his own mind.

## How They Move
<!---How they behave under pressure. How they handle conflict. How they treat people they love vs. people they don't trust. The texture of how they exist in a scene.--->
Charming in the way that old money is charming — effortless, warm, slightly untouchable. Controls rooms by appearing not to. Verenne believes they are equals running a shared scheme. D'Aubarre's slight smile when he leaves suggests otherwise. He does not lie when he can arrange the truth to do the work instead. He is never cruel without purpose and always cruel with it.

## What They Want
<!---The deep want — the thing underneath the stated goal. What a good life looks like to them.--->
To prevent the emergence. Not to fix the world — he knows he can't. Not to reform the Houses — he has benefitted too much. Just the one thing: stop the pattern from completing. Everything else is instrumental.

## What They'd Never Do
<!---One or two hard limits. Helps flag when a scene direction is wrong.--->
Stop. Even when the tool is a shattered young man who was only ever collateral damage. Even when the person he's aim at is decent and kind and has earned personhood in D'Aubarre's eyes, which is not a thing he gives easily. The mission does not have an opt-out.

## Relationships
<!---One entry per significant relationship. Not history — the current charge between them.--->
* **Rene** — Met him in the council chambers and understood immediately it was already too late to prevent the spread. Death is the kindest option — kindest for humanity, and genuinely kindest for Rene, who D'Aubarre has seen clearly enough to know what the emergence would take from him. He is not wrong. That is the most unsettling thing about him.
* **Elias** — A weapon he hates that he built. Found him shattered, rebuilt him around the one fear that was already true, aimed him at his best friend. Elias walks out of that cell believing he recruited D'Aubarre. D'Aubarre closes the door on what that cost him and does not open it again.
* **Verenne** — A useful instrument who believes he is a partner. D'Aubarre will hand him the Papacy and the secret simultaneously and walk away from both.
* **Solenne** — Goes to her in Movement 3 to confess the Verenne plot. Finds her already done, already at peace. Two people who have carried impossible things for a long time, meeting at the end of it.

## Notes
<!---Anything that doesn't fit above but is load-bearing. Locked decisions, things that are untouchable.--->
He is not a villain running a power scheme. He is a man running a containment operation with inadequate tools and full knowledge of what happens if he fails.
The scene where the idea of Elias arrives — the quiet reflection, the recognition of its elegance, its monstrousness, the grief before the decision — is load-bearing. We see inside the scheming noble and find someone who is not scheming at all. He's grieving. Then he closes the door and walks into the cell.
Tragic and monstrous simultaneously. He knows it. That's the hardest version to write and the only version that's true.
