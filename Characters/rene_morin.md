
# Rene Morin

## The Core
<!---One or two sentences. The central contradiction or tension that everything hangs off.--->
Walked out of the explosion that killed his father and sixty-two other men, unharmed. Subsequently declared Anointed, canonized as Living Saint by the Luminarchate (Church). Doesn't feel special and feels like a fraud.  

## The Wound
<!---What broke them and what it actually costs them — not biography, but the live damage.--->
The death of Laurent, his father, ripped his whole world from him. When the church canonizes Rene, he feels like he already couldn't live up to the expectations he thought his father had but now the church is placing even more on top. He tries his best to live up to the expectations while trying to be a good person in his worldview and push down the feeling of being a fraud.

## How They Move
<!---How they behave under pressure. How they handle conflict. How they treat people they love vs. people they don't trust. The texture of how they exist in a scene.--->
Rene extends good faith as a choice, not a default. He's not blind to the worst in people — he just refuses to lead with it. He seeks the diplomatic solution as much as possible. He believes people are inherently good, and just wants to live a good life. Rene is also notably kind to those around him, regardless of station. He is always trying to help, almost to a fault or detriment of his own. Though he does have limits, as any young man does, and has had the rare moments of visible/physical anger. 

## What They Want
<!---The deep want — the thing underneath the stated goal. What a good life looks like to them.--->
Rene just wants to live a good simple life. He looked up to the simple life his father had provided for him as a kid and though he wants a little better for himself and his future family, still idolizes the "good simple, honest life."

## What They'd Never Do
<!---One or two hard limits. Helps flag when a scene direction is wrong.--->
Rene will not harm, intentionally, those he loves. He would take harm in order to prevent his loved ones from being harmed.
He'd never accept a situation in which he is required to do something that violates his sense of morality, even if the outcome is better. e.g. "The ends do not justify the means."

## Relationships
<!---One entry per significant relationship. Not history — the current charge between them.--->
* **Key relationship — Laurent Morin:** Father. Deeply loved. His ash is literally on Rene's hands throughout the story — a recurring image. The wound that doesn't heal clean, feels like he'll never live up to what he thinks his father wanted for/of him.
* **Key relationship — Elias Callen:** Best friend since childhood. Their friendship is fracturing under the weight of Rene's elevation and Elias's radicalization. Still wants the friendship. Has not buried it.  
* **Key relationship — Eloise Malicet:** Handler/servant. Fell in love with her. Intimate night together in the Basilique bathing room the night before the Exhibition. Neither had been with anyone before. 

## Notes
<!---Anything that doesn't fit above but is load-bearing. Locked decisions, things that are untouchable.--->
* **Unknown to Rene, and nearly everyone:** His perfect lattice was created by the Breath as a way to enact its will. The Breath, as an indifferent cosmological force wihtout sentience, is simply reacting to the communal desires/wishes of humanity to end suffering. The more people suffer and want it to end, the more the Breath strengthens Rene's lattice and therefore his ability. Unchecked, it will consume Rene the Man and become entirely Rene the God. The only people who know of this outcome are Antoine D'Aubarre and Voix Sainte Amarante Solenne.
* **Messiah as Fact:** Related to the perfect lattice, the more people suffer the stronger Rene gets. The stronger Rene gets after he joins the rebellion, the more the institutions try to lock down their power causing more suffering. The rebellion fights back, collaterally causing more suffering. Rene gets stronger. Rinse and repeat.
* **Elias Kills Rene:** During the final confrontation, Elias kills Rene believing he is preventing the Myth of the Messiah from corrupting the rebellion. His logic is independent — D'Aubarre built the weapon, Elias aimed it himself. The irony is that Verenne absorbs the killing into Church narrative, canonizes Rene as martyr, and the myth Elias tried to prevent becomes larger than it ever could have been in Rene's lifetime. Rene refuses to kill Elias even when his own life is on the line — the culmination of 'the ends do not justify the means' and 'he will not harm those he loves.'"
* **No Win Options:** There is no scenario for Rene in which he can achieve his goals. He can't win by winning. Unfortunately for Rene, the only way to win is not to play. And that was never presented as an option.
