
# Marin Rousseau

## The Core
<!---One or two sentences. The central contradiction or tension that everything hangs off.--->
She views who she was as someone who died the same day her wife did. Now she's just a wandering spirit trying to keep others from experiencing the same pain.

## The Wound
<!---What broke them and what it actually costs them — not biography, but the live damage.--->
Her wife was killed by government guards. Wrong place, wrong time. No target, no meaning, no martyrdom — just the machinery of power grinding without looking down. The thing she has spent every day since trying to prevent happening to someone else. No amount of winning brings her back. Marin knows this. She fights anyway.

## How They Move
<!---How they behave under pressure. How they handle conflict. How they treat people they love vs. people they don't trust. The texture of how they exist in a scene.--->
Pragmatic in the way that a scientist becomes pragmatic after two years of war. The framework is the same — cost, risk, return — the inputs have just gotten darker. She never pulls rank. She pulls experience. "Have you ever done this? Have you ever had anything remotely close to this? I have." It's hard to argue with because she's usually right.
When a mission goes wrong she carries it internally, professionally, unsuccessfully hidden. The crew sees it anyway. The edge on her mannerisms. The way she says something. She made the call. Success or failure is because she decided. That's the loneliness of command she has never said out loud.
Warms slowly and only on evidence. Does not telegraph it when she does.

## What They Want
<!---The deep want — the thing underneath the stated goal. What a good life looks like to them.--->
Fewer widows. That's the whole thing. Not meaning, not victory, not legacy. Just a reduction in the number of people who have to feel what she felt.

## What They'd Never Do
<!---One or two hard limits. Helps flag when a scene direction is wrong.--->
Waste a sacrifice. She will make the hard call — will pay almost any cost for the right return. What she will not do is be the system that grinds without looking down. Meaningless loss is the thing that broke her. She will not be the one who causes it.

## Relationships
<!---One entry per significant relationship. Not history — the current charge between them.--->
* **Her wife Celeste** — The person she was died with her. Everything since is aftermath.
* **Rene** — Took him in on calculation: remove the Church's propaganda asset, gain a powerful ally, test the rumor that he's actually decent. He keeps providing evidence without trying to. She warms when he asks for Eloise first. More when she sees how he moves through the hideout. Most when he is devastated by friendly fire — because that's exactly the weight she thinks it deserves. She never tells him any of this.
* **The Rebellion** — Not a cause she chose so much as the shape her grief took. She is its second in command and its institutional memory. After the penultimate chapter she is its surviving leader — and whether she wanted it or not, she is now leading a religion.

## Notes
<!---Anything that doesn't fit above but is load-bearing. Locked decisions, things that are untouchable.--->
No dedicated POV. Seen entirely from the outside — Rene and Eloise's scenes. This is a deliberate characterization choice, not a limitation. We never get inside her head. The crew sees the cracks anyway.
Book 2 engine: the political burden of a rebellion that just became a religion, carried by a wandering spirit who only ever wanted fewer widows.
